microcanonical ensemble

Premises

  • The fundamental assumption of statistical mechanics:

For an isolated system in equilibrium, all accessible microstates are equally likely.

Definition

Definition. \(\Omega(E)\) is the number of microstates with energy \(E\).

It follows that for a system with a known energy \(E\), the ensemble has nonzero probabilities only for states \(\left \lvert n \right \rangle\) with energy \(E\). The probability is given by

$$ P(\left \lvert n \right \rangle) = \frac{1}{\Omega(E)} $$

This is the microcanonical ensemble.

We’ll define the thermodynamic entropy to be

$$ S(E) = k_B \ln \Omega(E) $$

where we have used Boltzmanns Constant.

Compound systems and the Second Law

Non-interacting

Consider two non-interacting systems. The numbers of states are multiplied

$$ \Omega(E_1,E_2) = \Omega_1(E_1) \Omega_2(E_2) $$

while the entropy is additive, making it an extensive quantity.

$$ S(E_1,E_2) = S_1(E_1) + S_2(E_2) $$

This product-to-sum relationship is a physical manifestation of the group homomorphism property of the exponential function.

With infinitessimal interaction

As soon as we turn on interactions the story changes. We keep it tiny so we can neglect changes in energy levels within the two subsystems.

There is now a dramatically larger number of possible states. While previously each subsystem state had a number of states associated with one particular energy, say \(\Omega_1(E_1)\), now there are not only those states but also the further multiplicity associated with the ability to choose all the different ways to distribute the total energy among the two subsystems.

Formally, there is of course a subtlety about which energies \(E_i\) are actually allowed, depending on the discrete spectra of the subsystems. For now we continue the fast-and-loose analysis.

We can express this increased state count as

$$ \begin{aligned} \Omega (E_\mathrm{Total}) &= \sum_{A_i} \Omega_1(E_i) \Omega_2(E_\mathrm{Total} - E_i) \\ &= \ ... \ + \ \Omega_1(E_1)\Omega_2(E_2) \ + ... \ \\ & \ \geq \ \Omega_1(E_1)\Omega_2(E_2) \end{aligned} $$

Thus we find that entropy is certain to increase as well, once we allow intersystem interaction to time-evolve the compound system.

$$ S (E_\mathrm{Total}) = k_B \ln (\Omega_\mathrm{Total}) \geq S_1(E_1) + S_2(E_2) $$
