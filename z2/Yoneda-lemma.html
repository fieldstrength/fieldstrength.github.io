Yoneda lemma

Covariant version

Theorem. For any functor \(F : C \to \mathrm{Set}\) from a locally small category \(C\), and for each element \(c \in C\), there is a bijection

$$ \mathrm{Hom}(C(c,-), F) \cong Fc $$

Furthermore, the bijection is a natural isomorphism in two distinct ways, i.e. considering both sides as functorial in either \(F\) or \(c\).

Note that the Hom-set in the LHS is in the category of functors \(C \to \mathrm{Set}\), whose arrows are natural transformations. So there is a bijective correspondence between the set \(Fc\) and the set of natural transformations between the covariant hom functor \(C(c,-)\) and \(F\).

So, writing it another way,

$$ \big\{ C(c,-) \Longrightarrow F \big\} \cong Fc $$

I’ve decided that the Yoneda lemma is the hardest trivial thing in mathematics

- Dan Piponi

Proof

Proof of bijection

We can directly construct the two sides of the bijection \(\Phi, \Psi\) as follows (with some extra type signatures to clarify what’s happening):

$$ \Phi : \mathrm{Hom}(C(x,-), F) \to Fx $$

$$ \bbox[8px,border:2px solid #DAB5E5]{ \Phi (\alpha) \equiv \alpha_x(1_x) } $$

$$ \Psi : Fx \to \mathrm{Hom}(C(x,-), F) $$

$$ \Psi(z): \mathrm{Hom}(C(x,-), F) $$

$$ \Psi(z)_d : C(x,z) \to Fz $$

$$ \Psi(z)_d(f) : Fz $$

$$ \bbox[8px,border:2px solid #DAB5E5]{ \Psi (z)_d (f) \equiv Ff(z) } $$

liftYoneda :: Functor f => f a -> Yoneda f a
liftYoneda fa = Yoneda (\h -> fmap h fa)

lowerYoneda :: Yoneda f a -> f a
lowerYoneda (Yoneda h) = h id

Discussion

Bartosz Milewski:

The Yoneda lemma is about category theory eating its own tail. The tail in this case is set theory. There is one special place in category theory where you have to use sets, and that’s the hom-set…

The Yoneda lemma is about functors from any category C to Set … you can think of F as “lossily” embedding C in Set.

But there is already a canonical way of representing a category [the hom functors]. So we always have a whole family of functors from C to Set – one for each choice of A. (That’s the tail-eating part)

Then there are functors that are isomorphic to those. They are called representable functors. The isomorphism in this case is an invertible natural transformation.

The Yoneda lemma tells us that all the remaining functors from C to Set are reachable through natural transformations from those representable functors.
