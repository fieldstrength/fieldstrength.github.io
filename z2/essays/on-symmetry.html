symmetry

Symmetry is one of those ideas that pervades everything.

The most prominent area where we encounter it is in understanding the univese, where it has been a persistent theme in all the major developments of the last century. But we also see its reflections in a number of other areas: symmetry’s power comes from its implications in math, so we see it in other domains where math plays a role such as computer science, economics, game theory, machine learning.

Besides having wide applicability, symmetry’s impact is also deep. It can be deployed as a very useful technical tool to solve very concrete problems, but it also informs the very basic ways we identify and conceptualize the elements of these domains. It is a tool for intuition and philosophy as much as it is for hard-nosed analysis.

Here I will try to shine a light on how symmetry informs the very identity of the concepts we know from our world. And I’ll try to do it while highlighting the concrete ways it has influenced, and been influenced by, dramatic advances in understanding the physical world.

Examples of symmetry principles in physics

Our familiar 3D space

To start off, lets choose the most familiar possible example of a symmetric object.

For many, the first thing that comes to mind in describing our space is that it can be described with 3 numbers. It is 3-dimensional. While this is surely an important aspect, it is other properties of our space that bear special emphasis. Namely, what makes our space more than merely a collection of 3 numbers.

One of the most basic properties of our space is that we can change our perspective within it. We can translate or move our position within it, and we can rotate to change our orientation.

Such operations will have a special role to play. We are essentially describing functions

Obviously not all systems composed of 3 numbers can have this property.
