gamma matrix

The Dirac gamma matrices capture the structure of spin. They are both spin quantum [observables](observable)❌ and generators of rotations. They occur in the Lagrangian and equations of motion for fermionic particles.

The construction of gamma matrices shows how [quantum mechanical](qm)❌ spin vectors in higher dimensions are described by an additional qubit for every two more dimensions.

Equational properties

They are square matrices whose dimension is a power of two.

They satisfy the Clifford Algebra

$$ \bbox[8px,border:2px solid #DAB5E5]{ \{ \Gamma^\mu, \Gamma^\nu \} = 2 \eta^{\mu \nu} } $$

and also commute with the Lorentz-group generators

$$ \Sigma^{\mu \nu} = \frac{-i}{4}[\Gamma^\mu, \Gamma^\nu] $$

Ladder operators, basis states

Define \(k + 1\) sets of raising/lowering operators. We can think of each one as acting on a particular “qubit” comprising the overall vector.

$$ \Gamma^{0 \pm} = \frac{1}{2} \Big( \pm \Gamma^0 + \Gamma^1 \Big) $$

$$ \Gamma^{a \pm} = \frac{1}{2} \Big( \Gamma^{2a} \pm i \Gamma^{2a+1} \Big) $$

where \(a \in \{1, ... k\}\)

These satisfy

$$ \{ \Gamma^{a+}, \Gamma^{b-} \} = \delta^{ab} $$

$$ \{ \Gamma^{a+}, \Gamma^{b+} \} = 0 $$

$$ \{ \Gamma^{a-}, \Gamma^{b-} \} = 0 $$

In particular, since every such operator anticommutes with itself, every “qubit” value can only be raised one time.

Consider the spin state \(\zeta\) with all its qubits maximally lowered.

Construction in arbitrary dimension

Even dimensions

Let the spacetime dimension be

$$ d = 2k + 2 $$

First, we consider minkowski signature \(SO(d-1,1)\).

The gamma matrices can be constructed by iteratively applying [kronecker multiplication](kronecker-product)❌. Starting from \(d=2\) we have the first two gammas:

$$ \Gamma^0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$$ \Gamma^1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Then each time we add a pair of dimensions we double the size of our matrices and add two more. First we tensor the existing gammas with a 2x2 like this:

$$ \Gamma^\mu = \gamma^\mu \otimes \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Then we add the two new matrices, by tensoring with the \(d-2\) identity:

$$ \Gamma^{d-2} = I \otimes \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$$ \Gamma^{d-1} = I \otimes \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Chirality

Define the chirality matrix

$$ \Gamma = i^{-k} \prod_{\mu=0}^{d-1} \Gamma^\mu $$

As the name suggests, it essentially captures the parity of the spin state, i.e. it takes a value of -1 iff the qubits have an even number of -1/2 factors and +1 otherwise.

it satisfies

$$ (\Gamma)^2 = 0 $$

$$ \{ \Gamma, \Gamma^\mu \} = 0 $$

$$ [ \Gamma, \Sigma^{\mu \nu} ] = 0 $$

Odd dimensions

Simply include \(\Gamma\) or \(-\Gamma\) as the final gamma matrix.
