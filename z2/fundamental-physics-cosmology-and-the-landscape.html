Examples of Naturalness

Classical radius of the electron

Abraham and Lorentz thought the electron was point-like particle. Consider its electrostatic field

$$ \vec{E} = \frac{e\hat{r}}{r^2} $$

And consider the energy from this electric field

$$ U = \int d^3 r E^2 = \int d^3 \frac{e^2}{r^4} \backsim e^2 \int \frac{dr}{r^2} $$

The divergent integrand would need to be modified at some small distance scale \(a\). If we consider simply cutting off the integral at this scale we get the contribution from the rest of the integral:

$$ U_a = \frac{e^2}{a} $$

The naturalness consideration then says that this scale \(a\) should not be so small that it overwhelms the relativistic rest-mass energy of the electron, because it would require an unlikely coincidence for the energy from the new physics to be just such as to cancel this electric field energy, to result in an exponentially smaller value for the final physical energy of the state. Thus the prediction becomes that there should be new physics emerging by around the distance scale \(a\) given by

$$ \frac{e^2}{a} = m_e c^2 $$

$$ a = \frac{e^2}{m_e c^2} $$

The prediction bears out. In fact, even before this distance scale we get the quantum-mechanical appearance of anti-electrons…

$$ \frac{\hbar}{m_e c} > \frac{e^2}{m_e c^2} $$

$$ 1 > \frac{e}{\hbar c} = \alpha $$

Instead in the expanding universe

The effective “interaction distance” is proportional to the number density and its cross section. To estimate the relic abundance we consider this comparable to the hubble distance $$ \ell_\mathrm{int} \sim \frac{1}{n \sigma} \sim \frac{1}{H} $$

Now using the assmption that we expect this to happen with \(T \sim m\)

$$ n \sigma \sim H \sim \frac{T^2}{M_\mathrm{Planck}} = \frac{m^2}{M_\mathrm{Planck}} $$

$$ \rho_\mathrm{WIMP} = n m = \frac{m^2}{M_\mathrm{Planck}\sigma} $$

We have a measured value of the cosmological parameter $$ \frac{\rho}{T^3} = 10^{-3} eV $$

Which in this scenario determines the cross section

$$ \sigma \sim (10^{-17} \mathrm{cm})^2 $$

By dimensional analysis reasons we expect the relevant diagrams to be proportional to

$$ \sigma \sim \frac{g^4}{m^2} $$

Consider a theory in 2D Euclidean space. We will tend to write the metric like

$$ ds^2 = dx^2 + dy^2 $$

Its rotational invariance is sort of obvious, but not manifest in this form. We can make it manifest by going to polar coordinates

$$ ds^2 = dr^2 + r^2d\theta^2 $$

where

$$ \begin{aligned} x &= r \cos \theta \\ y &= r \sin \theta \\ \end{aligned} $$

Now its clear that our metric is invariant under additive shifts in theta

$$ \theta \mapsto \theta + C $$

How is locality seen in the S-matrix?

5:00

The [[S-Matrix]]❌ is the only rigorous quantum gravity observable on flat space.

How is the property of locality manifested in the S-matrix?

Consider a function \(f\) that is nonzero on a finite interval (say [0,1]). How is that property reflected in the Fourier transform \(\tilde f\)?

  • \(\tilde f(p)\) is analytic in \(p\). Because its a sum of analytic functions and non-analyticity comes from contributions at \(\pm \infty\).
  • Its exponentially bounded

$$ \tilde f(p) \lt e^{-|p|} $$

9:30

So on the actual amplitude, the direction of the inequality flips. The conditions are

  • \(\cal M\) is analytic in \(s,t\) except on the Real line, where it may have cuts.
  • At high energies \(\cal M\) is exponentially bounded. From initial considerations, by \(e^{-E}\). With more careful treatment the bound is actually

$$ {\cal M} \geq e^{-E \ln E} $$

Since the S-matrix is the fundamental observable, we should reverse this reasoning: These properties should be the definition of locality.

Compare to quantum field theory amplitudes which are far away from this bound: finite powers of energy

Compare to string theory amplitudes: High energy fixed-angle scattering is dominated by a high genus surface (comparable to the energy in string units) and this contribution exactly saturates this bound.

Therefore, string theory is local.
