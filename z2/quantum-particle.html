quantum particle

The scientific framework of quantum mechanics can be applied to a wide range of dynamical systems, of finite or infinite dimension, but the simple quantum particle moving in \(D\) dimensions is the prototypical example of a continuous degree of freedom under quantization. And it is the one of most practical relevance, at least until we start describing processes involving relativistic speeds or particle creation/destruction.

Kinematics

In accordance with the postulates of quantum mechanics, the kinematical state space of the quantum particle is the Hilbert space of norm-1 complex-valued vectors. (or at least bounded so we can rescale them)

Unlike in classical mechanics, there are no extra variables for momentum. The position-space and momentum-space wavefunctions are just unitary transforms of each other.

classical state spacequantum state space
\(\mathbb R^{2D}\)\(\{ \mathbb R^D \to \mathbb C \ \mid \ \rm{Normed}\}\)

Measurements meanwhile are represented by self-adjoint linear operators acting on the Hilbert space of states. Instead of a vector of real numbers, the quantum position operator is a vector-valued operator in which each component scales the input wavefunction value by the value of the corresponding spatial coordinate.

The quantum momentum operator by contrast is mapped to the gradient of the wavefunction times a constant.

$$ \vec{p} \to -i \hbar \vec\nabla $$

This is equivalent to the de Broglie formula.

$$ p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{2\pi \hbar}{\lambda} $$

Predictions

In QM the best we can do is compute an amplitude for our outcome, which we can turn into a probability.

In general, such amplitudes are computed by sandwiching the operator between the vector and corresponding covector representing our state (letting bra-ket notation notation guide us):

$$ \left \langle \psi \right \rvert \hat O \left \lvert \psi \right \rangle $$

Dynamics

In a general quantum system dynamics are specified by the time-dependent Schrödinger equation.

$$ i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \hat H \Psi $$

So we need to characterize the Hamiltonian operator to determine the how to time-evolve the system. Thus we need to look at the functional form of the energy of the classical system and apply our canonical substitution rules to obtain the corresponding Hermitian operator.

The energy takes a contribution from the inherent kinetic energy of the particle in question, which classically is written

$$ \tfrac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{p^2}{2m} $$

We can also add a potential energy contribution. In general this expression can be an arbitrary real function of the position \(V(\vec x)\) (Imaginary values can encode some degree of probability non-conservation, i.e. by processes that can remove or introduce particles). Most of undergrad level QM consists of solving the system dynamics for different forms of \(V\).

So we have an expression for the Hamiltonian operator:

$$ H[\Psi(\vec x)] = \bigg[ V(\vec x) + \frac{-\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \bigg] \Psi(\vec x) $$

Note we have introduced an operator \(\nabla^2\) that is a shorthand for the quantum version of the classical operator, i.e.

$$ \nabla^2 = \sum_{i=1}^D \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i^2} $$

Separation of variables

It will turn out that this approach is sufficient to obtain general solutions of the TDSE.

Consider solutions of the form

$$ \Psi(\vec{x}, t) = \psi (\vec x) \varphi (t) $$

We can plug this in to TDSE, while using the product rule on our product solution. This drops terms where the “wrong” derivative operator is acting on one side of each product.

$$ i \hbar \frac{d \varphi}{dt}\psi(\vec x) = V(\vec x) \varphi(t) \psi(\vec x) + \frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 \psi(\vec x) \varphi (t) $$

Then dividing the whole equation by \(\varphi \psi\) we get an equation in which one side has all the \(t\) -dependence and the other side has all the space dependence. The only way this can be correct is if the whole thing is constant. With some foresight we can call this separation constant \(E\).

$$ i \hbar \frac{d \varphi}{dt} \frac{1}{\varphi(t)} = V(\vec x) + \frac{-\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{1}{\psi(\vec x)} \nabla^2 \psi(\vec x) = E $$

So we get two separate equations for spatial and time-dependence. Re-arranging the spatial equation slightly we can recognize it as the eigenvalue equation for the Hamiltonian operator with eigenvalues \(E\).

$$ \bigg[ V(\vec x) + \frac{-\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \bigg] \psi(\vec x) = \hat H \psi (\vec x) = E \psi(\vec x) $$

Meanwhile for a give value of \(E\) the time-dependence part of the equation

$$ \frac{d \varphi}{dt} = \frac{-iE}{\hbar}\varphi $$

Singles out a simple complex phase to evolve the wavefunctions components with each energy eigenvalue.

$$ \varphi(t) = \exp(-i E t / \hbar) $$
