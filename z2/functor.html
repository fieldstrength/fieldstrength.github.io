functor

A functor is a mapping between categories that preserves compositional structure. That is, it is a category homomorphism.

Functors are a major way that category theory describes connections between different areas of mathematics.

The definition is motivated by the need to describe natural transformations.

Definition

A covariant functor \(F\) between categories C and D is written $$ F : C \to D $$

This implies a mapping of both objects and morphisms of C. The name \(F\) may be overloaded for this purpose.

  • The mapping preserves identities

$$ F (1_{x \in C}) = 1_{Fx \in D} $$

  • The mapping preserves composition

$$ F (g \circ f) = F g \circ F f $$

Classifications

For locally small categories \(C,D\), a functor \(F : C \to D\) defines a function:

$$ F_{x,y} : C(x,y) \to D(Fx,Fy) $$

  • \(F\) is faithful if \(F_{x,y}\) is injective.
  • \(F\) is full if \(F_{x,y}\) is surjective.

Examples

Consider for example a representation of a group. This is a functor from the group considered as its own category to \(\mathbf{Vect_{\mathbb{C}}}\).

In programming

A variety of functors appear in programming. Especially as endofunctors.
