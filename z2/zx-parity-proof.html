ZX parity proof

This is a rough note about an approach to proving Kochen Specker theorem.

Definitions

Detector schemes: The \(\Sigma\) matrices

  • Start with 3 basic elements (0-cells?) describing single-qubit operations

$$ \Sigma \equiv \big\{ I, X, Z \big\} $$

These map to the 3 single qubit operations, including two Pauli matrixes plus identity

$$ \big\{ I, \sigma_x, \sigma_z \big\} $$

  • Multi-qubit words, which are associated with tensor product operations in the multi-qubit Hilbert space. For example

$$ XXZZ \in \Sigma^4 $$

Really they will indicate not just this multi-qubit observable but the set of observables including this one and all it’s constituent single-qubit observables.

  • Sets of \(\Sigma^n\) operators.

Let \(\Sigma^n_m\) represent a set of \(m\) \(\Sigma^n\) operators.

Interpretation

Properties

The \(\Sigma\) elements map to operators that are not closed under multiplication. If we include a complex phase however, then it works.

Its sufficent to introduce phases as multiples of \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\), which have the group structure of the cyclic group \(Z_4\).

Commutation

Lemma. The commutator of any two elements of \(\Sigma^1\) is either zero or proportional to their product.

Proof: Proceeds by direct calculation on the Pauli matrixs. There are essentially 3 cases:

  • Any element commutes with the identity: \([s,I] = [I,s] = 0\)
  • Any element commutes with itself: \([s,s] = 0\)
  • For any \(s \neq I\) and \(t \neq I\) then \([s,t] = st - ts = 2st\)

This in particular implies that

$$ \forall s,t \in \Sigma^1 , \ \ st = \pm ts $$

Lemma. For any two elements \(s,t \in \Sigma^n\), either

  • \(st = ts\), OR
  • \(st = -ts\)

Proof: The interchange law of monoidal categorys applied to Hilb says: The product of two multi-qubit operators composed by tensoring single-qubit operators is the same as the tensor product of each product of single-qubit operators.

Theorem. For multi-qubit operators \(s,t \in \Sigma^n\), they commute \([s,t] = 0\) iff there are an even number of single-qubit subspaces (i.e. columns) in which there is a non-identity mismatch. i.e. where \(s_i \neq t_i\) and neither \(s_i\) nor \(t_i\) is equal to \(I\).

Definition. The duplication operation

$$ \mathrm{dup} : \Sigma^n \to \Sigma^{2n} $$

$$ \mathrm{dup}(s) = s \otimes s $$

Theorem. The duplication operation on any \(\Sigma_m^n\) yields a mutually commuting set of observables.

Proof: Using the last theorem and the fact that

$$ \mathrm{odd} + \mathrm{odd} = \mathrm{even} $$

$$ \mathrm{even} + \mathrm{even} = \mathrm{even} $$

Correlation

Proof of Kochen-Specker

For any \(\Sigma_m^n\) satisfying the following:

  • For every pair of rows there are an even number of non-identity mismatches
  • In each column each non-identity observable (\(X\) or \(Z\)) occurs an even number of times
  • The product of all observables is minus the identity.

Then a proof is furnished…
