entanglement

Entanglement is an property of generic quantum states of a compound physical system, representing their inability to be expressed as the product of states of the constituent systems.

Definition. A quantum state is separable if it can be written as a tensor product

$$ \left \lvert \Psi_{12} \right \rangle = \left \lvert \Psi_1 \right \rangle \otimes \left \lvert \Psi_2 \right \rangle $$

Definition. A quantum state is entangled iff it is not separable.

Entanglement can also be considered a resource.

Basic properties

Constraints on coefficients

Assume we have a separable state. What constraints does that imply about the wave function components?

$$ \psi = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} c \\ d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ac \\ ad \\ bc \\ bd \\ \end{pmatrix} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \\ \delta \\ \end{pmatrix} $$

By comparing (dividing) the different components with a shared factor, we discover the following constraint

$$ \alpha \delta = \beta \gamma $$

So, to reiterate, quantum states satisfying this equation are the non-entangled states.

Entanglement entropy

A key property of entanglement is the von Neumann entropy it induces in the reduced density matrix of each subsystem, making it described by a [[mixed-state]]❌.

See entanglement entropy.

Entanglement classification

In two qubits there is one way for a state to be entangled, the Bell state.

In three qubits there are two ways for a state to be entangled, with prototypes the GHZ state and the w state.

Entanglement geometry

Consider a pure state \(\lvert \psi \rangle\) in the composite system of two qubits. There is an entanglement-sensitive function on it that can identified with the [Hopf-map](Hopf-fibration)❌ (the projection of the Hopf fiber bundle)

$$ S^7 \to S^4 $$

Specifically we have

$$ \psi \mapsto \Big( x_0, x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4 \Big)^T $$

where

$$ \begin{aligned} x_0 &= \left \langle \psi \right \rvert \sigma_z \otimes \mathbb{I} \left \lvert \psi \right \rangle \\ x_1 &= \left \langle \psi \right \rvert \sigma_x \otimes \mathbb{I} \left \lvert \psi \right \rangle \\ x_2 &= \left \langle \psi \right \rvert \sigma_y \otimes \mathbb{I} \left \lvert \psi \right \rangle \\ x_3 &= \Re \left \langle \psi \right \rvert \mathbf{E} \left \lvert \psi \right \rangle = 2 \Re (\alpha \delta - \beta \gamma) \\ x_4 &= \Im \left \langle \psi \right \rvert \mathbf{E} \left \lvert \psi \right \rangle = 2 \Im (\alpha \delta - \beta \gamma)\\ \end{aligned} $$

$$ \mathbf{E}\psi = (\sigma_y \otimes \sigma_y ) \psi^\star $$

The results of the previous section imply that iff the state \(\left \lvert \psi \right \rangle\) is entangled, then \(x_3, x_4\) are nonzero.

Furthermore the resulting 5D vector is normalized \(\sum x_i^2 = 1\), assuring us that it is in fact a point on \(S^4\). Quoting Chruściński:

a state is separable iff its image under the Hopf map belongs to the equatorial 2D sphere defined by the intersection of \(S^4\) with the 3D plane \(x_3 = x_4 = 0\).

Entanglement and locality

Entanglement is perhaps most famous for its tension with relativity. Namely by seeming to entail causation that reaches outside the lightcone, violating the speed of light limit imposed in special relativity.

There are a number of ways to understand or (arguably) resolve this tension.
