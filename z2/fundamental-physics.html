fundamental physics

Concerns the fundamental operation and configuration of nature.

At the present time, the frontier of knowledge on fundamental laws chiefly pertains to the [unification] of gravity and quantum mechanics, while the overall configuration and history of our universe is the subject of cosmology. In general, unification is necessarily a major theme of fundamental physics as, unlike other areas of physics, it does not suffice to have a theory with a limited domain of applicability.

The topic does not just concern the discipline of physics, as basic questions in philosophy, especially ontology and epistemology, are involved in framing this quest to understand reality. To even speak the term “fundamental physics” itself pretty much implies certain assumptions about those questions; assumptions that are reasonable and useful to adopt, but ones worth naming and defending regardless.

premesis and basic questions

A basic framing question is whether nature even obeys “laws” at all. It may be the case that nature does not operate according to precisely definable rules. In fact much discussion of the question seems to take the liberty to outright assert it does not. I would challenge the unqualified assertion that nature does not operate by any rules – at the very least this is a live possibility. Moreover the “unreasonable effectiveness of math in physics” seems to provide compelling evidence that nature does in fact operate by definable rules. However, a reasonable counter-hypothesis is that this could be explained instead by a selection effect.

In practice, work in fundamental physics does not need to assume nature obeys fundamental laws absolutely. It merely assumes that nature obeys laws to close enough approximation for us to be able to form useful [models](model)❌. If it did not, there would be no point and no way to proceed with the project of physics as currently conceived.

Structural Realism

the map and the territory

the mapping

Just like in computation with denotational semantics we have an idealized model we want to map to something in the real world, in fundamental physics we consider a mapping from a [[mathematical-structure]]❌ to (whole or part of) [physical reality]. We can refer to such a mapping as a [realization].

In both cases, our effort is mainly directed at making the model match as precisely as possible.

In the case of science, we proceed by iterting the scientific model to improve the match as much as possible.

By contrast, in computer science / software engineering, we actually iterate both sides of the mapping, predominantly at different phases of software development. In the semantic design of the program, we try to find an appropriate model matches the world in such a way that we do the right things to provide the intended value. During implementation, we instead try to make the realization in our physical computer match the model, the spec, we designed.

Risks of conflation

In a situation where we use a model to reason about something in the real world, there is some risk of confusing properties of the model for properties of the world or vice-versa.
