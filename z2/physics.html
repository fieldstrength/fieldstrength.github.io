physics

Nature is a strange beast, and understanding it is rife with challenge.

My interest in particular often centers on [fundamental physics].

premises

We can broadly categorize the most basic questions about a physical theory into

  • [kinematics] – What are the basic structures or entities involved?
  • [dynamics] – How do those basic entities change? How can input data be turned to predictions?

Of course there is much to say about how we relate the theory to practical life, either for understanding or experimental investigation. For example, methods of error analysis and statistical inference are crucial for scientific progress. There are also non-trivial questions about interpretation of the theory, especially with interpretation of quantum mechanics – how we map it to everyday experience.

major themes

The most significant insights and developments in physics have to do with [unification]. This often goes hand in hand with the prominent role of [symmetry].

framing

As a branch of science, physics is concerned with making scientific models of the world and using input from experiments to cull incorrect models and to guide the construction of more-correct models and the understanding built upon them.

Even the most basic aspects of how we frame the project of physics have proven resistant to conclusive answers because developments in understanding nature have themselves undermined our notions about how science and physics ought to proceed!

from classical framing to two modern pillars

From the standpoint of assumptions in [classical mechanics], physics has no trouble to accommodate a very traditional conception of how science works. We choose a mathematical structure to encode the state of the world, and given an element of this kinematical state-space we should be able to derive the exact state of the world at some future [time].

Both major revolutions of the 20th century, [quantum mechanics] and [general relativity], force us to depart from this basic picture in highly consequential ways.

quantum

At the small scales, like around \(10^{-10}\) meters, matter acts as a kind of wave. A wavefunction, whose values encode probabilities of outcomes of possible measurement. This is [quantum mechanics]. Some of these wavefunctions are valued in countable, finite sets such as spin up and spin down \(\{ \left \lvert \uparrow \right \rangle , \left \lvert \downarrow \right \rangle \}\) while others are valued continuous sets, like space. The fact that the theory outputs predictions in the form of [probabilities](probability)❌ was a major development.

relativistic

Secondly, the nature of space and time is modified in the presence of significant relative speeds or significant mass. Time is nearly like another dimension of space, whose definition depends on a particular observer’s perspective. Space and time can be rotated into one another, just like dimensions of space can, when changing vantage point. This is [relativity].

Both of these two pillars, which utilize radically different fundamental concepts, have the beginnings of a convergence in [quantum field theory]. Namely it combines [quantum mechanics] with [special relativity].

the quest to unify

A fully satisfactory unification of the two pillars has yet to be settled. It is also one of the main problems that suggests not merely a need for new scientific model (going beyond the Standard Model) but an entirely new scientific framework (going beyond quantum field theory+general relativity). This is the major unsolved problem in fundamental physics.

We have achieved a robust and versitile unification of quantum mechanics and special relativity in the form of [quantum field theory], with its concrete implementation in the [Standard Model] currently the best match for experimental data. That leaves [gravity], as described by [general relativity] as the last holdout. In particular, this means we need a theory of [quantum gravity].

Even with current knowledge, there is a rich interconnection between ideas of quantum field theory and general relativity. For example, starting with basic ideas of quantum mechanics and special relativity, with certain conditions we can derive that a massless spin-2 field requires the diffeomorphism symmetry of general relativity as is required to decouple timelike modes of the field, in the similar way to how a massless spin-1 field requires the gauge symmetry of [electromagnetism] or [Yang-Mills theory] to decouple its timelike mode. Furthermore, general relativity has no trouble to be treated in low-energy regimes as an [effective field theory], with the limits of this approach manifesting as the non-renormalizability of the field theory.

However, this success should not be taken to imply that normal quantum field theory is a sufficient foundation for quantum gravity, which poses deep mysteries of a fundamental and conceptual nature from this starting point. For example holography, and the fact that we don’t even have satisfactory answers as to what a quantum gravity theory should predict.

information and complexity considerations

Even with an exceedingly good description, obtaining useful predictions is often only possible in special circumstances. With either a sufficiently sall, or sufficiently lage, number of primative ingredients, predictions becomes computationally feasible. That is to say, that physics is not only concerned with forms of mechanics, but with [thermodynamics].

why I care

I think the ways the universe works are incredibly beautiful, but besides that there are a number of ideas that are valuable to repurpose.
