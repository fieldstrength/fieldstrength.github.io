classical mechanics

Classical mechanics describes much of the physical phenomena of common experience. Within classical mechanics we can [unify](unification)❌ the motion of human-scale objects like baseballs and airplane with motions of celestial objects like the earth and moon, with the experience of coarse [thermodynamic](thermo)❌ effects like heat or pressure.

It is noteworthy historically for essentially marking the beginning of modern science, with its basis in mathematics.

Formulations

Newton’s Laws

First Law

An object not experiencing any net force has constant velocity. $$ \vec{F} = 0 \implies \ddot{\vec{x}} = 0$$

Second Law

$$ \vec{F} = m \vec{a} = m \ddot{\vec{x}} $$

Put another way: The force equals the the change-rate of momentum

$$ \vec{F} = \dot{\vec{p}} $$

Third Law

If object 1 exerts force \(\vec{F}_{12}\) on object 2, then object 2 simultaneously exerts a reaction force $$\vec{F}_{21} = - F_{12}$$ on object 1.

Lagrangian mechanics

Fully characterizing the state of a classical mechanical system requires specifying not just positions but also momenta (i.e. speeds).

Consider our system to be parametrized by generalized coords \(q_i\) and generalized speeds \(\dot{q}_i\).

Lets define the Lagrangian as a function of these coordinates, equal to kinetic minus potential energy.

$$ L = T - U $$

And we defined the action as the time integral of the Lagrangian.

$$ S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q,\dot{q}) $$

$$ \delta S = 0 $$

Consider a 1D particle moving in a constant potential energy landscape.

$$ L(q, \dot{q}) = \tfrac{1}{2} m \dot{q}^2 - U(q) $$
