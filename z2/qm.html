quantum mechanics

QM is a scientific framework that generalizes classical mechanics.

It is noteworthy for departing from its predecessor in not being deterministic (at least in practice), but rather giving predictions only in the form of probability of allowed outcome. This was a radical departure from the expectations that were traditionally placed on a scientific theory or scientific framework.

Postulates

Operator formalism

  • The state of a physical system corresponds to a vector in a complex Hilbert space, normalized to \(1\). 1 .

$$ \bbox[8px,border:2px solid #DAB5E5]{ \left \langle \Psi \vert \Psi \right \rangle = 1 } $$

  • There exists a Hermitian operator called the Hamiltonian that defines the time evolution for the physical states.

$$ \bbox[8px,border:2px solid #DAB5E5]{ i\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left \lvert \Psi \right \rangle = \hat H \left \lvert \Psi \right \rangle } $$

  • observables correspond to Hermitian operators. Measurement of the observable yields one of the corresponding operator’s eigenvalues.

Overview

Range of application

Since QM was first developed, many advances of our understanding have proceeded not by modifying this basic framework, but by changing the system we are applying it to. Most notably, incorporating special relativity and particle creation/destruction required changing from the basic models of quantum particles to quantum field theory.

While we know for sure that our current scientific theorys based on QM are incomplete, notably lacking an account of quantum gravity, it is not yet clear if the framework of QM itself must be modified or superceded. Notably string theory is a singularly promising candidate framework for quantum gravity that does not modify QM itself.

Considerations for fundamental physics

Much of our understanding of QM comes from systematic ways to take a system in classical mechanics and quantizing it, and forming the quantum mechanical system out of it.

This is very useful and practical for most purposes, but for investigating big fundamental questions this perspective is quite likely not sufficent. After all, this approach has the ‘causality’ wrong, it is QM that is primary and classical mechanics represents a particular way of taking a limit of it.

Further, we have examples of theoretical quantum systems, like the (6,0) quantum field theory that dont have a known classical limit. While another quantum system, string theory evidently has six classical limits, namely the five superstring theories and 11-dimensional supergravity.

compositional properties

We can use ideas from category theory to understand and classify some of the aspects of compositionality in quantum mechanics. See categorical quantum mechanics.

Just like in classical mechanics, and in classical logic or computation (see algebraic data types), we can compose systems with essentially the operations AND and OR. Products and sums.

Of course, generally for scientific purposes we’re interested in more than just the pure tensor product or direct sum of consitituent systems (the two ways of composing specialized to quantum systems), because we need to add some kind of interaction between them in order for the other parts to matter to us.

In technical terms, this compositionality of quantum systems is captured by saying that quantum systems are described by a rig category. In other words, a category that has a notion of “addition” and “multiplication” defined on it, such that the multiplication distributes over addition. In other words, it is a monoidal category in two compatible ways, just like how a a standard ring in algebra has addition and multiplication operations that must distribute. We can think of this a a categorification of a rig (ring without negation), i.e. a 2-rig.
