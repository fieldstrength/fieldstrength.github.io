scientific framework

In the service of constructing a scientific theory, a framework means the general mathematical structure, which must be further supplemented by phenomenologically motivated data in order to be ready to make useful predictions.

Physics Examples

For example quantum field theory is a quite broad framework that can accommodate a huge variety of worlds that look nothing like our own. It describes an uncountable infinity of scientific models. Yet one specific model, the Standard Model is the one with direct scientific success. There are furthermore uncountably many ways of extending the SM even within the same framework of quantum field theory.

Similarly string theory is a physical framework from which specific models are developed. Unlike quantum field theory the additional data is not the value of continuous parameters, but rather, essentially a choice of vacuum.

Drawing Boundaries

In practice the distinction between a theory and a framework can get rather hazy.

For example, string theory is both a #scientific framework and a scientific theory, in the sense that different vacua are not completely disconnected propositions about nature because dynamical transitions between them are actually possible. At least a given superselection sector (i.e choice of asymptotic boundary conditions).

By the same token even the Standard Model is a sort of framework in a sense, because like a generic quantum field theory it seems to possess more than one vacuum. The other one would lead to radically different phenomenology than we observe.

Similarly classical mechanics and #quantum mechanics are frameworks.

Note on terminology

This is not standardized terminology, but its also not uncommon in physicist lingo.
